Daily Memo (06-Jun-2026)

Key Themes

  • Macro Looks Resilient But Risks Skewed to Downside: Headline US macro signals—payrolls, claims, retail sales—continue to show resilience, while easing financial conditions (Chicago Fed index steadily more negative) and cooling inflation (core CPI/PCE inching up but not accelerating) create a constructive short-term backdrop for risk assets. However, negative consumer sentiment, depleted emergency oil reserves, and margin risks from energy/geopolitics (see below) argue the soft landing narrative is running on borrowed time.
  • Energy Supply Shocks Gaining Teeth—Complacency in Energy, FX, and Treasuries: US oil inventories hit lows not seen since 2004 (market headlines), while WTI remains elevated—even as Gaza/Iran conflict risks linger. The potential for a true global pricing shock is underappreciated given still-firm USD and Treasury yields that have not yet adjusted for a supply-driven inflation impulse—markets are not pricing even modest disruption tail risk.
  • Geopolitics: Market Pricing Is Too Relaxed on War Fat-Tail: Despite near 50/50 probabilities in some event markets (“China invades Taiwan,” “Trump out before GTA VI,” etc.), the reality is that actual near-term military or presidential transition risk (by 2026) remains very low (markets closer to 5–7% for ’26 Taiwan scenario; main event probabilities are moonshot-bloated). However, ongoing Gulf/Middle East and Ukraine escalations inject persistent optionality into risk premia, especially for commodities, defense, and select EMFX.

Macro

Rates & Yields

  • 2Y yield hovering just above 4.0%, 10Y at 4.47%. Yield curve remains positive—10Y/2Y near +0.4%, steepening from flat/inverted 2025 lows.
  • Fed Funds Rate at 3.63% in May—market not pricing imminent easing, nor hike cycle resumption.
  • Chicago Fed Financial Conditions Index continues to ease (latest -0.494), risk asset-supportive—no broad credit stress, supports carry trades.

Labor

  • Nonfarm payrolls rise through April/May (159M), steady 200k+ monthly prints; unemployment at 4.3% (flat).
  • Jobless claims slightly elevated but not surging, JOLTS job openings rebounding (7.6M, up from Q1 soft patch)—real slack is not building.
  • Labor market resilient; no leading indicators of cyclic downturn, but consumer remains unconvinced (see below).

Consumer

  • Retail sales continuing sharp MoM expansions—no “real” deceleration after CPI/PCE adjustment.
  • Michigan Consumer Sentiment: fresh lows (49.8 in April), falling even as jobs and nominal wages hold up. Sustained disconnect: bolstered by higher energy/food costs, policy/political anxiety.
  • Net: US household demand still flows, but confidence shows latent vulnerability—if labor softens, falls could be sharp.

Inflation

  • Core CPI/PCE: headlined higher, but momentum is easing (core CPI April: 335.4 from 334.2 in March; core PCE April: 129.63 from 129.32). No renewed acceleration, but not deflating either.
  • Oil, food, and border-driven ag shocks set up Q3-Q4 upside risk to headline prints.

Liquidity, Money & FX

  • M2 uptrend continues (April: $22.8T), mild post-2025 acceleration.
  • Trade-weighted USD above 119—stabilizing after brief run-up, still high by standards of past decade; no break in global capital demand yet.

Geopolitics
