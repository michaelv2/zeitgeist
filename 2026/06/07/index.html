Key Themes

  • The mispriced regime is stagflation-lite, not the hike-vs-cut debate the tape is obsessed with. CPI is re-accelerating before the oil shock even hits the data (core CPI +0.38% MoM in April, headline +0.64%; March headline +0.86%) — tariff pass-through into core goods is a plausible driver of that firmness, to be confirmed by subsequent prints, not transitory energy. Core PCE, by contrast, is cooling at the margin (+0.24% MoM in April vs +0.40% in Feb) — a genuinely mixed inflation picture, not a unidirectional re-acceleration. Meanwhile the consumer is softening underneath the nominal strength (Michigan sentiment 49.8 vs 56.6 in Feb; April real retail sales roughly flat-to-slightly-negative once you deflate the +0.49% nominal by +0.64% CPI — one month, noisy, needs confirmation). Both legs disappointing — that's worse for cyclicals AND duration than either a clean boom or a clean slowdown. Confirm/refute: a hot June-10 CPI alongside soft June sentiment/real-spend prints.
  • Fed regime change under new Chair Warsh has turned the front end hawkish, and the 2Y is the tell. The 2Y at 4.05% sits ~40bp above an effective funds rate of 3.63% — the front end has priced out cuts and tilted toward higher-for-longer with some hike risk. The curve is bear-flattening hawkishly (10Y–2Y down to 0.38 from 0.49 in two weeks), with the front end leading the selloff. Duration (ZROZ/TLT) and the highest-multiple AI cohort are the pain trades into FOMC June 16–17; Nasdaq just printed its worst day of the year (−4.2%).
  • Trump's "US equity stake" in AI giants is an unpriced overhang on the exact cohort that just broke. A government profit-share/equity claim on the most expensive names in the market is a structural de-rating risk landing right as positioning unwinds — with no confirmed meeting date yet, this remains a live headline risk rather than a dated catalyst.

Macro

Inflation — mixed picture, CPI firming while PCE cools

  • Core CPI accelerated in April (+0.38% MoM, ~4.6% annualized) — this predates the May oil spike, so tariff pass-through to goods is a plausible driver, though one that needs subsequent prints to confirm as sticky rather than a transient blip.
  • Core PCE momentum is, by contrast, cooling at the margin (April +0.24% MoM, ~2.9% annualized vs +0.40% in Feb) — the one dovish thread, but it's the lagging series and won't print until June 26.
  • May CPI (June 10) gets an added energy kicker from the Iran oil spike even though crude has since reversed — expect a hot headline that reinforces the no-cut stance.

Rates — front end pricing higher-for-longer with hike risk

  • Fed holds June 16–17; with Warsh hawkish and data hot, a cut is off the table and the front end is pricing higher-for-longer with some hike risk (2Y 4.05% > funds 3.63%).
  • Hawkish bear-flattening (10Y–2Y at 0.38) with the front end leading signals the market expects policy to stay tight enough to slow growth — a policy-error/late-cycle signature, not a reflationary one.
  • Read: underweight long duration into June CPI + FOMC. The eventual duration trade is after the hawkish panic peaks, not into it.

Labor — "hot" headline on a flat trend

  • The 172k May print overstates reality: payroll levels have grown only ~59k/month on average over nine months (158.5M→159.0M), with multiple negative months in between.
  • Continuing claims sticky-elevated (~1.78M) — the unemployed are staying unemployed longer, even with unemployment pinned at 4.3%.
  • Read: labor is treading water, not booming — which is exactly why the inflation re-accel in CPI is so problematic (no growth offset).

Consumer — the soft spot

  • Sentiment collapse is the loudest signal (Michigan 49.8, down from 56.6 in Feb) — gas-price spike + sticky inflation biting.
  • April nominal retail +0.49% was roughly flat-to-slightly-negative in real terms — one noisy month that needs a second soft real print to confirm an income squeeze rather than a dip. Watch real retail as the key tell.

Housing

  • Home prices flattening/rolling over (Case-Shiller 332.0 Feb → 331.3 Mar, first sequential decline) under a 4.47% 10Y. Cap rate pressure on REITs (VNQ); no relief until the long end falls.
